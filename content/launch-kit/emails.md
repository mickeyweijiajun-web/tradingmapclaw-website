**Scenario setup (illustrative, sample data):** Say the system is running a research cycle on a generic large-cap technology asset in Group B (holdings-style tracking group). This is a walkthrough of mechanics, not a real-time read on any specific asset.

**Pass A — Fundamentals (Engine 1, weighted 40%):** Engine 1 (Hermes Agent) pulls fundamentals, valuation multiples, and recent insider-activity data, and produces a preliminary read. In this illustrative cycle, let's say the fundamentals pass lands on a bullish scenario label, driven by valuation metrics relative to sector peers.

**Pass B — Technicals + cross-verification (Engine 2, weighted 35%):** Engine 2 (Codex) works independently on technicals, capital flow, and options positioning — and its mandatory second job is to cross-verify the numbers Engine 1 used in Pass A. In this illustrative example, say Engine 2's independent check of the valuation inputs matches Engine 1's figures, but the technical and capital-flow read comes out neutral, not bullish.

**Pass C — Macro/sentiment (Engine 1, weighted 25%):** Engine 1 layers in a macro, industry, and sentiment pass. Say this lands neutral as well, given mixed sector-wide signals.

**The council step:** Because Pass A (bullish) and Pass B (neutral) disagree in direction, the Multi-Model Council — DeepSeek V4 Pro, GLM-5.2, GPT-5.6 — reviews the disagreement rather than letting one pass silently override the other. In this illustrative cycle, say the council doesn't reach unanimous consensus, so the fixed tiebreaker order applies: DeepSeek's read is weighted first, then GLM-5.2, then GPT-5.6.

**The output:** The final report doesn't hide that Pass A and Pass B disagreed — it shows both, plus the weighted blended scenario label (in this illustrative example, landing at neutral overall, given the 40/35/25 weighting and the fundamentals-technicals disagreement). That's the entire point of the architecture: disagreement is visible in the output, not smoothed away before you ever see it.

1. **Pass A — Fundamentals (Engine 1, Hermes Agent, weighted 40%):** valuation, fundamentals, insider activity. This is the primary analytical pass.
2. **Pass B — Technicals + cross-verification (Engine 2, Codex, weighted 35%):** technicals, capital flow, options — run independently of Engine 1, plus a mandatory check of Engine 1's numbers. 5. **Output:** a weighted scenario label — bullish, neutral, or bearish — plus visibility into whether the passes agreed or disagreed along the way.

I want to underline the two things that make this different from a single-model tool: first, Engine 2's cross-verification of Engine 1's numbers is mandatory, not optional — nothing ships without it. Second, disagreement between passes is shown in the output, not resolved invisibly before you see it.

None of this produces advice. It produces a structured, checked research read that ends in a scenario label, and you decide what to do with that information, if anything.
